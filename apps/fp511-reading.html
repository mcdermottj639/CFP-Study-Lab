Step 1: Understanding the Client's Personal & Financial Circumstances

−
Quantitative (objective) data: Assets, liabilities, income, expenses, tax returns, insurance policies, investment statements, wills, trusts, 401(k) statements, partnership agreements, employee benefits, business information
Qualitative (subjective) data: Goals, values, health, risk tolerance, attitudes, family circumstances, life expectancy assumptions, return assumptions, personal beliefs and priorities
Incomplete information rule: If unable to obtain sufficient and relevant quantitative information, the planner must either (1) restrict the scope of the engagement to matters for which sufficient information is available, OR (2) terminate the engagement. The planner may NOT proceed with inadequate data.
Approved documentation methods: CRM software, handwritten notes, and emails. Voice memos are NOT approved.

Steps 2–7: Goals Through Monitoring

−
  • Step 2 — Identify & Select Goals: Collaborate to identify, select, and prioritize goals. Discuss unrealistic goals openly. Establish personal assumptions (life expectancy, retirement age) and economic assumptions (inflation rate, expected returns).
  • Step 3 — Analyze Current Course & Alternatives: Identify financial strengths (adequate savings, appropriate emergency fund, well-defined goals, proper insurance, valid estate documents) and weaknesses (insufficient savings, undefined goals, poor cash flow, misaligned investments, missing documents). Compare current course to alternatives.
  • Step 4 — Develop Recommendations: Select the best course(s). Consider assumptions, basis, timing, priority, and interdependencies with other elements of the plan. Recommendations must maximize potential to achieve client's goals.
  • Step 5 — Present Recommendations: Present clearly, including assumptions, risks, pros/cons, alternatives, anticipated effects, time sensitivity. Must be specific and actionable — vague recommendations violate the Standards.
  • Step 6 — Implement: Unless excluded from scope, the planner IS responsible for implementation. Identify appropriate products and services. Disclose conflicts of interest at this stage.
  • Step 7 — Monitor Progress & Update: Establish monitoring responsibilities. Communicate what is monitored, how often, and the client's duty to inform the planner of changes in circumstances. Review at least annually or when circumstances change materially.

CFP® Marks Usage Rules

−
  • Always capitalize: CFP® (never “cfp”)
  • Never use periods: CFP® (never “C.F.P.”)
  • Always use the ® symbol: CFP® (never “CFP” without ®)
  • Use with approved nouns unless directly following certified individual's name: certificant, professional, practitioner, certification, mark, exam
  • Correct: “Jane Doe, CFP®” or “Jane Doe is a CFP® professional”
  • Incorrect: “C.F.P.” / “CFP” (no ®) / “Jane is a CFP” (no ®)
  • CERTIFIED FINANCIAL PLANNER®: Always capital letters or small-cap font; always ® symbol; minimum 0.5 inch print or 50px screen; maintain clear space; must use with approved nouns unless following individual's name
Practice exam trap: “John Doe, CFP®” (correct) vs. “John Doe, CFP” (missing ® = incorrect) vs. “John Doe, C.F.P.” (periods = incorrect)

Planner, Client & Team Responsibilities

−
Planner responsibilities:
  • Identify financial goals and objectives
  • Understand current financial status
  • Analyze current course and alternatives
  • Recommend appropriate tools and strategies
  • Implement the plan (unless excluded from scope)
  • Monitor whether client is following through
  • Provide competence or refer (Standard A.3)
Client responsibilities:
  • Provide accurate, complete, timely information
  • Inform planner of material changes in circumstances
  • Follow through on agreed-upon implementation steps
Standard A.13 — Working with additional persons: When engaging or recommending another professional, the CFP® pro must (1) have a reasonable basis for the recommendation based on reputation, experience, and qualifications, and (2) disclose any arrangement by which someone other than the client will compensate the CFP® pro for the engagement. Note: Unless a CFP® certificant is also a licensed attorney, they may NOT draft legal documents or offer legal opinions.

Risk Assessment Framework EXAM

−
TermDefinitionKey Distinction
Risk ToleranceThe tradeoff a client is willing to make between potential risks and rewardsWillingness (subjective)
Risk PreferenceA client's attitude toward risk — a more constant personal traitStable over time
Risk PerceptionThe subjective judgment a client uses when evaluating the risk of a decisionCan be distorted by biases
Risk CapacityThe degree to which a client's financial resources can mitigate (absorb) riskAbility (objective/financial)
Risk LiteracyThe ability to comprehend and act on financial risk informationKnowledge and skill
Loss aversion is related to the fear of regret. Risk tolerance reflects the willingness tradeoff; risk capacity reflects financial ability. Both must be considered — a client may be willing to take more risk than their capacity allows.
  • Visual learners: Prefer graphs, charts, pictures; present financial data visually
  • Auditory learners: Prefer hearing/speaking; discuss information before putting it in writing
  • Kinesthetic learners: Hands-on; write goals and steps as they are formulated together
Values vs. Attitudes: Values are attitudes and beliefs for which a person feels strongly; they represent what a person believes to be right. Attitudes reflect a person's opinions, values, and wants — attitudes shape values but are not the same.

−

Cognitive biases stem from faulty reasoning or information processing. They can be corrected with coaching and better information.

Emotional Biases (7) EXAM

−

Emotional biases stem from impulse, feeling, or intuition. They are harder to overcome and often must be accommodated rather than corrected.

BiasDefinitionConsequence
Loss AversionFearing losses more than valuing equivalent gains; related to fear of regretWon't sell at a loss; “get-even-itis”; hold losers too long
OverconfidenceOverestimating own abilities; believe knowledge can control random eventsExcessive trading; undue risk; underestimate downside
Self-ControlFavoring immediate gratification over long-term goalsInsufficient retirement savings; overspending
Status QuoComfort with existing situation; resisting changeHold an inappropriate portfolio risk level indefinitely
EndowmentValuing owned assets more than their market valueOvervalue what is already owned; reluctance to sell
Regret AversionInaction from fear of being wrongParalysis; herding behavior; never pull the trigger
AffinityInvestment decisions based on personal connection or identityInvest in a company for emotional reasons (Stuart and Collegiate Construction)
Key distinction: Cognitive biases can be corrected with data/education. Emotional biases are rooted in feeling — sometimes the best strategy is to work within them rather than fight them.

Client Psychology Obstacles & Money Conflict

−
  • Money scripts: Unconscious attitudes about money formed in childhood; resistant to change; often passed through generations
  • Financial comfort zones: The financial range in which a client feels content — both floor (anxiety below) and ceiling (guilt above)
  • Financial socialization: How family, culture, and religion shaped money beliefs from childhood forward

Pathological Financial Behaviors

  • Affluenza (financial dependence): Fear that the supply of money will stop
  • Financial enabling: Supporting another at one's own financial risk
  • Hoarding disorder: Retaining goods beyond their useful life

Goal Incongruence Steps (Couples)

When couples disagree on financial goals: (1) Normalize the disagreement, (2) find common ground, (3) encourage sharing of perspectives. Partners should have transparency about all income, assets, liabilities, and spending — even with separate accounts.

Communication Principles

  • Body language is the most impactful form of communication
  • Lower pitch = confidence; higher pitch = uncertainty
  • Physical mirroring: Copy body language to build rapport
  • Verbal mirroring: Imitate client's word use and tone
  • Open-ended questions elicit reliable psychological profiles; closed-ended questions are used to clarify or confirm specific facts
  • Active listening: Give full, undivided attention. If distracted, apologize and ask client to begin again. Do NOT proceed on incomplete information.

Social Penetration Theory (Altman & Taylor) — 4 Stages

1. Orientation
First meeting. General conversation. Build initial likeability and trust. Client may speak in generalities. Goal: determine if relationship has value.
2. Exploration
Discuss planning process in more detail. Learn client's beliefs, attitudes, feelings. More in-depth but still comfortable. Foundation for trust is building.
3. Affective Exchange
Significant trust. Open disclosure, sincerity, intimacy. Relationship may become friendship. Client shares true feelings without reservation.
4. Stable Exchange
Deep empathy; planner anticipates questions and feelings. Long-term relationship. Honesty, trust, and mutual benefit. Not all relationships reach this stage.

Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet) EXAM

−
Assets = Liabilities + Net Worth   therefore   Net Worth = Assets − Liabilities
Asset Categories:
  • Cash & cash equivalents: Checking, savings, money market accounts, CDs with maturity ≤90 days
  • Invested assets: Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, IRAs, 401(k)s, investment real estate
  • Personal-use assets: Primary residence, autos, boats, jewelry, furniture
Valuation rules:
  • Assets: fair market value
  • Liabilities: outstanding principal balance (not total future payments)
  • Credit card purchases: recorded as a liability on the balance sheet — NOT as a cash outflow until actually paid
Titling abbreviations: S1 = Spouse 1  |  S2 = Spouse 2  |  JT = Joint tenancy  |  CP = Community property

Cash Flow Statement

−
Net Cash Flow = Total Income − Total Outflows
Order of outflows (pay yourself first):
1. Savings & investments (listed FIRST — “pay yourself first” principle)
2. Fixed outflows (rent/mortgage, insurance premiums, loan payments)
3. Variable outflows (food, clothing, utilities, entertainment)
4. Taxes (income taxes + FICA/payroll taxes)
  • Credit card purchase: NOT a cash outflow at purchase time; it is a liability on the balance sheet. The cash outflow occurs only when the credit card bill is actually paid.
  • Savings ratio: Target 10–15% of gross income directed to savings and investments
  • Surplus = positive net cash flow (income exceeds outflows); deficit = negative (outflows exceed income)

Emergency Funds, Budgeting & Savings Strategies

−
3 months
Both spouses employed or single with significant secondary income
6 months
Single wage earner or married with only one income source
  • Appropriate emergency-fund investments: Checking, savings, money market deposit accounts, money market mutual funds, CDs ≤90 days
  • NOT appropriate: Stocks, bonds, or IRAs (illiquid or volatile). Lines of credit are a backup only.
  • Zero-based budgeting: Every dollar of income is assigned a purpose; total budget = total income. Forces deliberate spending decisions.
  • Envelope method: Physical or digital envelopes for each spending category; spending stops when envelope is empty
  • Pay yourself first: Savings are automatically deducted before discretionary spending is allocated; most effective savings strategy

Debt Management Strategies

−
Debt Snowball: Pay minimum on all debts; direct extra cash to the smallest balance first. Once paid off, roll that payment to the next smallest. Benefits: psychological wins, motivation, quick early victories. Cost: pays more total interest.
Debt Avalanche: Pay minimum on all debts; direct extra cash to the highest interest rate first. Mathematically optimal: saves the most total money. Risk: larger debts may take long to eliminate, reducing early motivation.

  • Rate-shopping inquiries: Multiple mortgage or auto-loan inquiries in a short window are treated as a single inquiry to avoid penalizing consumers who shop for the best rate
  • Do NOT close old accounts — closing reduces available credit (raises utilization ratio) and shortens length of credit history; both hurt the score

Mortgage Analysis

  • PMI required when down payment <20% (LTV >80%). VA loans: no PMI, no down payment. FHA: 3.5% down minimum, PMI required.
  • Refinancing break-even: Total closing costs ÷ Monthly savings = months to break even. Only worthwhile if staying beyond break-even point.
  • Negative amortization: Monthly payment is less than accruing interest; unpaid interest added to balance (ARMs, graduated-payment mortgages)
  • Reverse mortgage: Lender pays the homeowner. Age 62+. Nonrecourse loan. If in nursing home >12 months, home is sold. Subject to HECM rules.
  • Auto lease interest: NOT deductible for personal use; IS deductible for business use. Closed-end lease: walk away at end (possible mileage/damage charges). Open-end lease: may owe residual-value shortfall.

Calculator Fundamentals & TVM Variables

−
N
Number of compounding periods
I/YR
Interest rate per period
PV
Present value (today's value)
PMT
Payment (periodic amount)
FV
Future value
Sign convention: Outflows (money paid out) are entered as NEGATIVE. Inflows (money received) are entered as POSITIVE. Mismatched signs are the most common cause of calculation errors.
Always clear the calculator before every new problem (DOWNSHIFT + C ALL on HP10bII+; 2ND + CLR TVM on BAII Plus). Residual values from prior problems cause incorrect answers.
END mode (default) = Ordinary Annuity: Payments occur at the END of each period. Used for: mortgages, car loans, most investment problems, loan amortization.
BEG mode = Annuity Due: Payments occur at the BEGINNING of each period. Used for: leases, tuition payments (due at start of semester), insurance premiums.
Annuity due has a LARGER future value than an ordinary annuity because each payment earns one extra period of interest. This is always true and is frequently tested.

Basic TVM Calculations with Examples EXAM

−

Rule of 72

Years to double = 72 ÷ interest rate  |  Required rate = 72 ÷ desired years
Example: 9% rate → 72 ÷ 9 = 8 years to double

Compounding & Discounting

  • Compounding (FV): Earning interest on interest — money grows over time. The higher the rate or the more periods, the greater the growth.
  • Discounting (PV): Finding today's value of a future amount. The higher the discount rate, the lower the present value.
  • $150,000 at 7% for 5 years FV: N=5, I/YR=7, PV=−150,000, PMT=0 → FV = $210,383
  • Ordinary annuity FV example: $2,000/yr at 10% for 18 years (END mode) = $91,198. Same in BEG mode (annuity due) = $100,318. Difference: $9,120 more for starting payments one period earlier.

Loan Amortization

−
Always use END mode for loan amortization — interest accrues on the principal balance from payment to payment.
$135,000 mortgage, 4.5%, 30 years:
Monthly payment = $684.03
Total interest over life of loan = $111,249.05
Month 1: principal = $177.78 | interest = $506.25 | balance = $134,822.22
  • Early payments: Heavily weighted toward interest; little principal. This reverses over time.
  • Late payments: Heavily weighted toward principal; little interest. This is why refinancing early in a mortgage maximizes savings.
  • Auto loan example (Jack): $28,000 at 3.25% for 5 years → monthly payment = $506.24; total interest = $2,374.40

Inflation-Adjusted Rate & Serial Payments EXAM

−
Inflation-adjusted (real) rate = [(1 + return rate) ÷ (1 + inflation rate)] − 1
Example: 7% return, 3% inflation → (1.07 / 1.03) − 1 = 3.88%
Always slightly LESS than the simple difference (4% − 3% = 4%). Never use simple subtraction.

Serial (Inflation-Adjusted) Payments Setup

  • Use the inflation-adjusted rate as I/YR
  • Enter the desired goal amount in today's dollars as FV (not the inflated future amount)
  • Solve for PMT (this is the first payment in today's dollars)
  • Actual first payment = PMT × (1 + inflation rate)
  • Each subsequent year's payment increases by the inflation rate
  • Serial payments start LOWER than level payments but grow each year — same purchasing power throughout

IRR, NPV & Uneven Cash Flows

−
IRR (Internal Rate of Return): The compound annual return at which NPV = 0. Outflows enter as negative; inflows as positive. CF0 = initial investment. If IRR > required rate, investment is attractive.
NPV (Net Present Value): PV of all future cash flows at the required rate minus the initial investment.
Positive NPV = return exceeds the discount rate (invest).
Negative NPV = return below discount rate (reject).
  • Uneven cash flows: Enter each period's cash flow separately using the CF function. Different from annuity problems where PMT is the same every period.
  • Step-by-step for IRR/NPV: CF0 = initial outflow (negative), then each subsequent period's cash flows, then compute IRR or enter discount rate and compute NPV.
  • Reinvestment rate assumption: IRR assumes reinvestment at the IRR rate itself; NPV assumes reinvestment at the discount rate. NPV is generally the superior decision criterion.

Code of Ethics — 6 Principles

−

The Code of Ethics comprises 6 principles that apply at all times to individuals holding the CFP® marks — not just when providing financial planning.

  • 1. Honesty, integrity, competence & diligence: Provide services honestly and with candor; not subordinated to personal gain. Competence = relevant knowledge and skill. Diligence = timely, thorough responses.
  • 2. Act in the client's best interests: The cornerstone of the fiduciary standard; all recommendations must prioritize client interests above personal or firm interests.
  • 3. Exercise due care: Apply prudent professional judgment with the care, skill, and diligence expected of a professional in similar circumstances (prudent professional standard).
  • 4. Avoid or disclose & manage conflicts: Material conflicts must be disclosed with sufficiently specific facts; followed by informed consent and ongoing management. Disclosure alone does NOT satisfy the duty — the conflict must also be managed.
  • 5. Maintain confidentiality & privacy: Protect nonpublic personal information. Exceptions: (a) ordinary business purposes and (b) legal/compliance purposes.
  • 6. Reflect positively on the profession: Conduct must enhance public trust in the CFP® marks and the financial planning profession.

  • Duty to Keep Current: Maintain competence through ongoing education and staying informed of developments

Fitness Standards & Procedural Rules

−
Absolute Bars (always bars certification):
Felony conviction for fraud, theft, embezzlement, tax crimes, perjury; professional discipline based on intentional/reckless fraud, theft, or dishonest conduct involving clients or employers; personal dishonesty; serious crimes against persons
Must File Petition for Fitness:
Prior (no-longer-current) bar/suspension; felony not rising to absolute bar; relevant misdemeanor; two or more bankruptcies; civil fraud finding; certain client-dispute thresholds; unsatisfied tax lien or judgment

Disciplinary Sanctions (Escalating)

For current CFP® professionals: Private Censure → Public Censure → Suspension (90 days to 5 years) → Interim Suspension → Revocation (permanent). Bars (Temporary/Permanent) apply to NON-CFP® professionals (candidates).

Private Censure: The ONLY sanction that is NOT publicly disseminated. All other sanctions may be made public.

Economic Concepts EXAM

−
Elastic goods Respond strongly to price changes (luxuries: boats, jewelry, cars)
Inelastic goods Little response to price changes (necessities: prescriptions, electricity, gasoline)
Complementary goods Demand for one affects the other (peanut butter & jelly; cars & gasoline)
Substitute goods One can replace another when price rises (chicken replaces pork)
GDP = C + I + G + NE
C = Consumption (~2/3 of GDP, the LARGEST component)  |  I = Investment (incl. new housing)  |  G = Government spending  |  NE = Net exports (exports − imports)
GDP vs. GNP: GDP = production within a country's borders regardless of ownership. GNP = production by domestically owned entities anywhere in the world.

Fiscal & Monetary Policy EXAM

−
Fiscal Policy — Congress + President
Tools: tax rates and government spending
Mnemonic: “fisCal” = Congress
Expansionary: lower taxes, increase spending
Contractionary: raise taxes, decrease spending
Monetary Policy — Federal Reserve (independent)
Tools: reserve requirements, discount rate, open-market operations
Expansionary: buy securities, lower discount rate, lower reserve requirements
Contractionary: sell securities, raise discount rate, raise reserve requirements

Interest Rate Hierarchy

  • Discount rate: Rate banks borrow directly from the Fed — the Fed directly controls this rate
  • Federal funds rate: Overnight inter-bank lending rate — the Fed targets and influences this rate through open-market operations
  • Prime rate: Set by commercial banks; typically ~3% above the federal funds rate; used for best-customer lending
Open-market operations (most important tool): Fed BUYS securities → money flows to public → rates fall (expansionary). Fed SELLS securities → money flows to Fed → rates rise (contractionary).

Business Cycle & Economic Indicators

−
Recession: Real GDP declines for 2 consecutive quarters (≥6 months). Depression: Real GDP declines for 6 consecutive quarters (≥18 months).
TypeDefinitionExamples
Leading IndicatorsChange BEFORE the economy changesHousing starts, new unemployment claims, stock prices, orders for durable goods, bond yield spreads, investor sentiment. Don't wait for the economy to improve before investing — stocks are leading!
Coincident IndicatorsChange WITH the economy (confirm current phase)Industrial production, personal income level, corporate profits
Lagging IndicatorsChange AFTER the economy has shiftedPrime interest rate, CPI for services, business/consumer loans outstanding, average duration of unemployment
Stagflation: High inflation + high unemployment + slow growth (e.g., 1970s oil shock). Very difficult to treat because fiscal and monetary tools to fight inflation make unemployment worse and vice versa.
Deflation investment strategy: Own U.S. Treasuries; avoid stocks, real estate, and gold (in severe deflation). Bond prices RISE as rates fall.

Consumer Protection Laws

  • Truth in Lending Act / Reg Z (CCPA): Standard APR disclosure; all credit terms disclosed before signing; 3-day right of rescission (can cancel within 3 business days); unauthorized credit card liability capped at $50

  • Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA): Caps pre-service debt interest at 6% for active-duty servicemembers
  • Identity theft: Report to the FTC (not SEC). Three major bureaus: Equifax, Experian, TransUnion. Consumer can place a fraud alert or credit freeze.

529 Gift Tax Rules & Superfunding

−
$19,000
Annual gift exclusion per donor per beneficiary (2025). Single: $19K. Couple: $38K per beneficiary per year.
$95,000
Single donor 5-year superfunding limit per beneficiary ($19K × 5). Couple: $190,000 per beneficiary every 5 years. File Form 709. No additional gifts to same beneficiary during 5-year period.
Superfunding mechanics: Donor makes one large lump-sum contribution of up to $95,000 (single) or $190,000 (couple) per beneficiary, treated as if made ratably over 5 years. Must file Form 709 to elect the 5-year spreading. No additional annual exclusion gifts to the same beneficiary during that 5-year period. If donor dies within 5 years, pro-rated amount is pulled back into the estate.

Case Study Approach

−
Case Facts Analysis Approach: Read the case facts. Identify all relevant data. Categorize by domain (insurance, investments, taxes, retirement, estate). Identify explicit and implicit financial goals. Identify strengths and weaknesses in each domain. Develop integrated recommendations.
Question Review Approach: Start with the question. Identify which domain(s) it tests. Pull relevant case facts. Apply the relevant rule, formula, or concept. Eliminate wrong answers. Select the best answer based on what the question actually asks.
Integration is key: Recommendations in one domain affect others. Example: a large 401(k) rollover may trigger a Roth conversion opportunity, affect Medicare IRMAA, and change RMD calculations simultaneously. Always think across domains.

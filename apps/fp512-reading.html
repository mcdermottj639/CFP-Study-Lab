Life Cycle Insurance Needs

−

Life insurance need peaks during accumulation (~ages 20-45). Health/disability needs grow through conservation (~45-60). LTC dominates distribution phase (60+).

Risk Terminology & Classification EXAM

−

Risk = possibility of loss  |  Peril = cause of loss  |  Hazard = condition that increases potential for loss

Test Tip: Relationship between risk, peril, and hazard is frequently tested. Example: oily rags (hazard) increase chance of fire (peril) that damages your home (risk).
Static Earthquakes, floods — regular, insurable
Dynamic Economic changes — NOT typically insurable
Pure Loss or no loss only — insurable
Speculative Chance of gain or loss — NOT insurable
Fundamental Affects large groups (recession, pandemic)
Particular Affects individuals/small groups (house fire)

Moral hazard: Unethical behavior to induce a claim (arson for insurance proceeds). Morale hazard: Indifference/carelessness because insurance exists (not locking the car). Adverse selection: Those most likely to claim seek coverage — controlled by underwriting.

Insurable Risk Requirements & Limiting Factors

−
  • Large homogeneous pool — law of large numbers enables actuarial prediction
  • Definite & measurable loss — time, place, and amount determinable
  • Fortuitous/accidental — must be accidental (life insurance exception: death is certain but timing is not)
  • Non-catastrophic to insurer — no single event destroys the insurer
  • Geographic distribution — spread exposure to avoid single-peril devastation
  • Insurable interest — Life: must exist at issuance only. Property: must exist at BOTH issuance AND time of loss.
  • ACV (Actual Cash Value) = Replacement Cost − Depreciation — limits insurer liability to actual loss

Other factors limiting insurer liability: Policy limits/face value, other insurance provisions, coinsurance clauses, deductibles, and subrogation rights.

Insurance Producers & Authority EXAM

−
Agent
Represents the insurer. Has express, implied, and apparent authority. Insurer is legally liable for agent acts within scope. Agent's knowledge is imputed to the insurer.
Broker
Represents the insured. Cannot bind the insurer. Knowledge stays between broker and client. For exam: broker has NONE of an agent's authority.
  • Express authority: Specifically conferred in writing in the agency contract
  • Implied authority: Not expressly granted but reasonably necessary to carry out duties (e.g., collecting premiums, delivering policies)
  • Apparent authority: Appearance of authority based on insurer's actions or words; insurer may be bound even if agent exceeded actual scope
  • Ratification: Insurer accepts premium with full knowledge of agent's unauthorized act — thereby binding itself to that act going forward and modifying agent's powers
Key rule: Negligent acts, omissions, and misrepresentations of the agent are imputed to the insurer when the agent acted within scope of authority.

Tort Law & Liability Exposures EXAM

−

Tort: A civil wrong causing physical, emotional, or financial harm. The wrongdoer = tortfeasor. People buy liability insurance to protect against unintentional torts (negligence).

Four Elements of Negligence (ALL must be present)

1. Duty
A legal duty is owed to the plaintiff
2. Breach
The duty was breached by the defendant
3. Damages
Actual damages occurred to the plaintiff
4. Proximate Cause
Uninterrupted sequence between breach and damages
  • Attractive nuisance: Property feature likely to attract and injure children (e.g., swimming pool). Owner must fence/lock to prevent harm.
  • Negligence per se: Duty of care is determined by a statute designed to protect the class of plaintiff from this type of harm. Other negligence elements still required.
  • Absolute (strict) liability: Responsible for ANY damages even without negligence — applies to extra-hazardous activities (wild animals, blasting). Liability and fault are NOT synonymous.
  • Vicarious liability: Held liable for acts of another (parent/child; employer/employee acting on employer's behalf, even on own time)

Contributory NegligencePlaintiff was negligent in any degreeBars ALL recovery (strictest; mostly replaced by comparative)
Comparative NegligenceReduces award by plaintiff's % of faultMost common today — partial recovery allowed

Underwriting, Loss Adjustment & Selecting Insurers

−

Underwriting: Process of evaluating applicants to determine whether and on what terms to accept risk. Agents perform field underwriting (initial screening). Insurers use morbidity (probability of sickness) and mortality (probability of death) tables to price policies.

  • Adverse selection: Those most likely to have losses seek coverage; underwriting, waiting periods, and exclusions control this
  • Subrogation: After paying a claim, insurer recovers from at-fault third party; prevents double recovery by insured
  • Salvage: Insurer's right to take damaged property after paying full claim (e.g., totaled vehicles)
  • Abandonment: Insured cannot abandon property to insurer and demand full value; insurer may accept or reject salvage
  • Collateral source rule: Damages not reduced even if plaintiff received compensation from another source (e.g., health insurance)
NAIC does NOT rate insurance companies. A.M. Best, Moody's, S&P, and Fitch are the rating agencies. NAIC compiles a watch list but does not assign financial strength ratings.

Regulation of the Insurance Industry

−

Insurance is primarily regulated at the state level. Three branches involved: legislative (laws), executive/administrative (insurance department), judicial (interpretation).

  • Paul v. Virginia (1868): Supreme Court ruled insurance is NOT interstate commerce — states regulate
  • US v. Southeastern Underwriters (1944): Reversed Paul v. Virginia — insurance IS interstate commerce subject to federal regulation
  • McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945): Congress responded: federal authority limited to antitrust and fair labor; states carry the major burden of regulating insurance
  • NAIC: All 50 state insurance commissioners. Develops model laws and accreditation standards. Has no power to force states to adopt recommendations. Primary goal: maintain state regulation to prevent federal takeover.

Insurance Contract Characteristics EXAM

−
  • Aleatory: Outcome controlled by chance; dollars exchanged are often substantially unequal
  • Adhesion: Prepared entirely by insurer; insured accepts or rejects as-is — no negotiation. Ambiguities construed against the drafter (insurer).
  • Conditional: Insurer pays only if conditions are met and a covered loss occurs
  • Unilateral: Only the insurer can be held legally liable; insured's only enforceable obligation is paying premiums
  • Utmost Good Faith: Both parties must disclose all material facts
  • Personal contract: Follows the person, not the property; cannot be assigned without insurer consent (except life insurance death benefit)
  • Indemnity contract: Reimburses only up to actual amount of loss; no profit from insurance. Exception: life insurance pays stated face amount.

Contract Dispute Doctrines & Good Faith

−
  • Waiver: Voluntary and intentional relinquishment of a known right by the insurer; cannot later deny coverage on that same basis
  • Estoppel: Insurer legally prevented from asserting a right because its prior conduct misled the insured to their detriment
  • Rescission: Contract voided from inception; both parties returned to pre-contract position; used for misrepresentation or fraud
  • Reformation: Court rewrites contract to reflect true original intent of both parties
  • Parol evidence rule: Written contract generally controls over prior oral agreements

Homeowners Policy Structure: Sections I & II

−
Section I — Property
  • Coverage A: The dwelling (structure + attached structures)
  • Coverage B: Other structures (detached garage, fence) ~10% of Cov A
  • Coverage C: Personal property (contents) ~50-70% of Cov A
  • Coverage D: Loss of use / additional living expenses
Section II — Liability
  • Coverage E: Comprehensive personal liability (BI and PD to others)
  • Coverage F: Medical payments to others; claim expenses; damage to others' property
Key rule: Coverages B, C, and D are priced as percentages of the Coverage A limit. The insured selects only the Coverage A dwelling limit.

Key Property Insurance Concepts

−
  • Replacement Cost vs. ACV: Replacement cost pays full rebuild cost; ACV = Replacement Cost − Depreciation. Replacement cost requires insuring to at least 80% of replacement value.
  • Coinsurance (80% Rule): If insured carries less than 80% of replacement cost, partial losses are paid proportionally.
    Formula: (Amount Carried ÷ Amount Required) × Loss = Insurer Payment
    Example: Home replacement cost $200K. Should carry $160K (80%). Insured carries only $100K. Loss = $20K. Payment = (100K/160K) × $20K = $12,500. Insured absorbs remaining $7,500.
  • Subrogation: After paying claim, insurer recovers from at-fault third party; prevents insured from collecting twice
  • Other Insurance Provision: Pro-rata or primary/excess coordination; insured cannot profit from a loss
  • Pair or set clause: If one item of a pair/set is lost, insurer pays only the difference in value, not the full replacement cost of the pair

Personal Auto Policy (PAP)

−
  • Part A — Liability: Bodily injury and property damage to third parties; minimum state limits required in every state
  • Part B — Medical Payments: Covers insured and passengers regardless of fault; no deductible or coinsurance
  • Part C — UM/UIM: Protects you when other driver has no (UM) or insufficient (UIM) insurance
  • Part D — Physical Damage: Collision (your fault or single-car) + Comprehensive (theft, weather, animals — other than collision)
  • Umbrella/Excess: Additional liability layer above auto and homeowners limits; recommended for high-net-worth clients

VL vs. VUL: Variable Life has a GUARANTEED minimum DB (premium split: part to DB, part to subaccounts). VUL has NO guaranteed DB — all premium goes to subaccounts. VUL lapses if subaccounts reach zero; VL's guaranteed DB survives.

EIUL / IUL: Indexed Universal Life Detail

−
  • Participation rate: % of index gain credited to policy. Example: S&P 500 gains 10%, participation rate 70% → 7% credited
  • Rate cap: Upper limit on credited rate. Example: 12% cap with 18% S&P gain → only 12% credited
  • Spread method: Fixed % subtracted from index gain. Example: 30% gain minus 2% spread = 28% credited
  • Point-to-point (ratchet): Compares index at start and end of interval; mid-period fluctuations ignored; locks in gain each year
  • High/low water mark: Uses highest (or lowest) value within credit period; protects against end-of-period downturns
  • Downside protection: If index declines, account value stays unchanged (floor at 0% credited — never negative)
How insurers manage EIUL: Invest in fixed-income products and buy call options on the index. The cost of these options explains why participation rates, caps, and spreads limit the upside.

Policy Provisions & Nonforfeiture Options EXAM

−
  • Grace period: Typically 30 days after missed premium before policy lapses
  • Reinstatement: Right to restore lapsed policy (usually within 3-5 years) with evidence of insurability + back premiums + interest
  • Free look: Right to cancel new policy within 10-30 days for full refund
  • Incontestability: After 2 years, insurer cannot deny claim for misrepresentation (except fraud)
  • Suicide clause: Within 2 years of issuance: insurer returns premiums only, no death benefit
  • Misstatement of age: Benefit adjusted to what the actual premium would have purchased at the correct age
  • Spendthrift clause: Prevents beneficiary from assigning/pledging death benefit before it is paid; protects from beneficiary's creditors

Nonforfeiture Options (Permanent Life Only)

Cash Surrender Value
Terminate policy, receive current cash value. Policy ends. Gain above basis is taxable.
Reduced Paid-Up
Use CV to buy smaller amount of same type of permanent insurance. No more premiums. Coverage continues for life.
Extended Term
Use CV to buy term with same face amount for as long as CV will purchase. Default if none chosen.

Dividend Options & Riders

−

Dividends are paid by mutual companies (not guaranteed; considered return of excess premium):

  • Cash: Receive as check. Taxable to the extent it exceeds total premiums paid.
  • Reduce premium: Apply to next premium due — popular choice
  • Accumulate at interest: Leave with insurer; interest credited is taxable annually
  • Paid-Up Additions (PUA): Buy small amounts of additional paid-up whole life — increases both DB and cash value. Most advantageous long-term.
  • One-year term: Use dividend to buy additional one-year term coverage

Key Riders:

  • Waiver of Premium: Premiums waived during total disability (typically after 6-month elimination period)
  • Accidental Death Benefit (ADB): Extra benefit if death is accidental (double or triple indemnity)
  • Guaranteed Insurability (GI): Right to purchase additional coverage at future dates without evidence of insurability
  • Accelerated Death Benefit: Advance on DB for terminal illness (≤12-24 months life expectancy), chronic illness, or critical illness
  • Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA): Automatically increases DB to maintain purchasing power
  • Return of Premium: Premiums returned if insured survives the policy term; higher cost

Life Insurance Taxation EXAM

−
  • Death benefits: Generally income-tax-free (IRC §101)
  • Cash value growth: Tax-deferred inside policy
  • UL withdrawals: FIFO — basis out first (tax-free), then gains (taxable)
  • Policy loans: Not taxable while policy in force. If policy lapses with outstanding loan, excess over basis is immediately taxable.
  • MEC: Fails 7-pay test. Withdrawals: LIFO + 10% penalty before age 59½
  • Transfer for value rule: Sale/transfer of policy for consideration may make death proceeds taxable. Exceptions: transfer to insured, insured's partner, partnership where insured is partner, or corporation where insured is officer/shareholder.
  • Group life $50K rule: First $50K employer-paid coverage is tax-free. Above $50K: imputed income using Table I rates, reported on W-2.

Life Insurance Needs Analysis & Policy Replacement

−
  • Multiple of Salary: 6x, 8x, or 10x salary. Simple but imprecise; varies by age and situation.
  • Human Life Value: PV of future after-tax earnings; capital utilization (principal depleted) approach
  • Capital Retention: Use interest only; preserve principal for heirs. Generates LARGER insurance need.
  • Capital Utilization: Deplete principal over time. Generates SMALLER need. Risk: beneficiary outlives the fund.
  • LIFE Method: Liquidation of debts + Income replacement + Final expenses + Education funding
Capital Utilization (BEG mode): I/YR = (1+return)/(1+inflation)−1; PMT = needed income; Solve PV
Capital Retention: PV = Annual Income ÷ Inflation-adjusted rate

Churning: Repeatedly switching policies to generate commissions — unethical, may be illegal. Twisting: Misrepresenting a competitor policy to induce replacement. State replacement regulations require specific disclosure forms and comparisons before replacing an existing policy.

Payout Options & Taxation

−
  • Straight Life (Pure Life): Maximum monthly income for life; nothing to beneficiary if annuitant dies early
  • Life + Period Certain: Payments continue for guaranteed minimum period (5, 10, or 20 years) to beneficiary if annuitant dies early
  • Life with Refund: If annuitant dies before recovering full investment, beneficiary receives remainder as cash refund or installment refund
  • Joint & Survivor: 100%, 66.7%, or 50% continues to second annuitant; lower payout than single-life
  • Fixed with COLA: Starts lower but increases with inflation; protects purchasing power
  • Pre-annuitization withdrawals: LIFO — earnings come out first, taxed as ordinary income + 10% penalty before age 59½
  • Penalty-free withdrawals: Most contracts allow 10% of account value per year without surrender charges (still subject to LIFO tax rules)
  • Annuitization — Exclusion Ratio: Separates basis (tax-free) from earnings (taxable) in each payment
Exclusion Ratio: Investment in Contract ÷ Total Expected Return × Payment = Tax-Free Portion per Payment
Example: $100K invested, $750/mo for 20 years. Expected return = $180K. Ratio = 55.6%. Tax-free = $416.67/mo; taxable = $333.33/mo.

Key Federal Health Insurance Laws EXAM

−
COBRA (1985): Continuation of health benefits for 18-36 months. Applies to employers with 20+ employees. Premium up to 102% of group cost. Gross misconduct termination is NOT a qualifying event — employee AND dependents are ineligible.
HIPAA (1996): Eliminated pre-existing condition exclusions for group plans IF: (1) employee had prior coverage for at least 12 months, AND (2) fewer than 63 days have elapsed since loss of prior coverage. Pre-existing condition = treated or diagnosed within 6 months before enrolling in new plan. Largely superseded by ACA.
PPACA / ACA (2010): No denial for pre-existing conditions, coverage to age 26, essential health benefits, no lifetime maximums, Marketplace subsidies. Individual mandate penalty eliminated 2019.
Recent Laws: No Surprises Act (2022) — bans surprise OON emergency bills. Inflation Reduction Act (2022) — caps Part D MOOP at $2,000 (2025); eliminated donut hole; allows Medicare drug price negotiation (2026).

Health Care Cost Example (Frank, $25K hospital stay):
Policy: $1,000 deductible, 20% coinsurance, $5,000 MOOP
Step 1: Frank pays $1,000 deductible. Remaining = $24,000.
Step 2: 20% of $24,000 = $4,800, but MOOP caps Frank at $5,000 total.
Step 3: Insurer pays $20,000. Frank's total out-of-pocket: $5,000.

Simple formula: (Expense − Deductible) × Insurer% = Insurer payment.
Example: $1,500 loss, $500 deductible, 80/20 plan → ($1,500-$500) × 80% = $800 insurer payment.

Medical expense deductibility: Unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI are deductible as an itemized deduction.

Part B enrollment: General enrollment period is January 1-March 31 annually. If a beneficiary cancels Part B, they cannot re-enroll until January of the following year and may incur a lifetime late enrollment penalty. Veterans should sign up for Medicare as soon as eligible.

Medigap Plans & IRMAA

−

10 standardized plans (A through N). Same plan letter = identical benefits regardless of insurer; only premiums differ. Cannot have Medigap AND Medicare Advantage simultaneously.

  • Plan F: Most comprehensive (covers all gaps including Part B deductible). Only available to those eligible for Medicare before January 1, 2020.
  • Plan G: Same as F but does NOT cover Part B deductible. Now the most comprehensive plan available to new Medicare enrollees.
  • Plan N: Covers most gaps; does NOT cover Part B deductible or excess charges; copays up to $20 office/$50 ER
  • Plans K & L: 50%/75% cost-sharing on Part B coinsurance; lower premiums, more out-of-pocket exposure
IRMAA: Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount. Higher-income Medicare beneficiaries pay surcharge on Part B and Part D premiums. Based on MAGI from 2-year prior tax return (2025 premiums based on 2023 MAGI). Thresholds: $106,000+ single; $212,000+ MFJ (2025). Beneficiary may APPEAL IRMAA for qualifying life-changing events (retirement, death of spouse, divorce, work stoppage).

−

Disability prevalence: A healthy 35-year-old has approximately a 21-24% chance of becoming disabled for 3+ months before retirement. DI insurance replaces approximately 2/3 (60-70%) of pre-disability income.

Elimination Period, Benefit Period & Renewability

−
Elimination Period
Waiting period before benefits begin: 30, 60, 90, or 180 days. Longer EP = lower premium. Most common: 90 days.
Benefit Period
How long benefits are paid: 2 years, 5 years, to age 65, or lifetime. Advisors typically recommend to age 65.
Benefit Amount
Typically 60-70% of pre-disability income. Participation limits prevent over-insurance.

Renewability Provisions (Best to Worst for Insured)

  • Noncancelable: Insurer CANNOT cancel, change benefits, or raise premiums as long as premiums are paid. Best protection; most expensive.
  • Guaranteed Renewable: Insurer must renew at insured's option to stated age, but MAY increase premiums for entire class (not individually)
  • Conditionally Renewable: Insurer may refuse renewal under certain conditions specified in policy
  • Optionally Renewable: Insurer has right to refuse renewal at any premium due date. Least favorable.

Occupational Classifications, Riders & BOE

−

Occupational classification is the primary underwriting factor. As professional classification increases: definition of disability broadens, benefit period lengthens, more riders available. Note: Some occupations (chiropractors, some dentists) have been downgraded by many insurers due to claims history.

  • Residual/Partial Disability Rider: Insured works at reduced capacity and receives proportional benefit. Formula: (Income Lost / Pre-disability Income) x Full Monthly Benefit. Typically available when income loss is 20-80%.
  • COLA Rider: Increases benefit amount during disability to keep pace with inflation
  • Guaranteed Purchase Option (GPO): Right to purchase additional coverage at future dates without new evidence of insurability
  • Social Insurance Supplement (SIS): Provides extra benefit if Social Security Disability is denied
  • Waiver of Premium: Premiums waived while receiving disability benefits
  • Presumptive Disability: Automatically qualifies insured as totally disabled for loss of sight, hearing, speech, or limbs — no elimination period required
Business Overhead Expense (BOE): Covers a small business owner's FIXED operating costs (rent, utilities, employee salaries, loan payments) while the owner is disabled. Benefit period: typically 1-2 years. Premiums are deductible to the business. Benefits are taxable. Appropriate for professional practices, NOT large corporations.

Long-Term Care Insurance (LTCI) EXAM

−
Benefit Triggers (must meet ONE): (1) Inability to perform 2 of 6 ADLs (bathing, dressing, eating, continence, toileting, transferring), OR (2) Cognitive impairment.
  • Coverage settings: Home care, adult day care, assisted living, nursing home (skilled and custodial)
  • Elimination period: Typically 30-90 days. Calendar-day EP (all days count) vs. Service-day EP (only days care is received count — less favorable; takes longer to satisfy).
  • Benefit period: 2-5 years typical; unlimited available at higher premium. Average LTC claim: 2-3 years.
  • Inflation protection: CPI-linked or compound 5% annual increase — critical for policies purchased years before need. Compound inflation protection preferred over simple.
Partnership Long-Term Care Insurance: State-sponsored program where LTCI benefits paid dollar-for-dollar offset assets that would otherwise need to be spent down for Medicaid eligibility. Example: $200,000 partnership policy benefits paid → client can protect $200,000 in assets while qualifying for Medicaid. Heavily tested.
  • Hybrid Life/LTC policies: LTC benefit accelerates life insurance death benefit; remaining amount paid to beneficiary at death. Eliminates use-it-or-lose-it concern of traditional LTCI.
  • PACE (Program of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly): Federally funded; comprehensive medical and social services for frail elderly who qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid; alternative to nursing home placement.
  • Qualified LTCI tax treatment: Individual premiums deductible as medical expenses above 7.5% AGI (age-based annual limits). Employer-paid LTCI: deductible to employer, not taxable to employee. Benefits: generally tax-free up to indexed limits.

Veterans Benefits

−

Eligibility: Active military, naval, or air service without dishonorable discharge. If enlisted after September 7, 1980: must have 24 continuous months of active duty service. Reserves/National Guard qualify only if called to active duty by federal order and completed full period.

  • VA Health Care: Serves 9+ million veterans annually; 1,200+ care locations. VA Medical Centers, CBOCs, Vet Centers, Community Living Centers. Care may be provided in veteran's home if approved.
  • VA + Private Insurance: Non-service-connected conditions billed to outside carriers; service-connected covered by VA. Veterans encouraged to maintain Medicare and private coverage.
  • Disability Compensation: Tax-free monthly payments for service-connected disabilities; rated 0-100% in 10% increments
  • VA Pension + Aid & Attendance: Means-tested for wartime veterans with limited income; Aid & Attendance supplement for those needing LTC assistance (nursing home, assisted living, or home health)
  • HSA/HRA for VA costs: Veterans may use HSA or HRA to pay for VA care for both service-connected and non-service-connected conditions
Important: If a veteran cancels Medicare Part B, they cannot re-enroll until January of the following year and may incur a lifetime late enrollment penalty.

Group Life Insurance & the $50K Rule EXAM

−
$50,000 Income Exclusion Rule: Employer-paid group term life insurance is income-tax-free to the employee for the first $50,000 of coverage. Above $50,000, the cost of excess coverage is imputed to the employee using IRS Table I rates and reported as W-2 income.
Imputed Income Formula: [(Coverage above $50K ÷ 1,000) × Table I monthly rate × 12] = Annual taxable income
Example: $210,000 total coverage → $160,000 above threshold. Table I rate $0.06/month for age band: ($160K ÷ 1,000) × $0.06 × 12 = $115.20 taxable income per year.
  • Discriminatory plan rule: If group term life plan discriminates in favor of key employees, those key employees must include the ENTIRE employer premium paid on their behalf as taxable compensation (not just the amount above $50,000)
  • Portability: Employee may convert to an individual policy within 31 days of leaving employment, without evidence of insurability
  • No medical exam: Group life typically does not require individual medical underwriting for eligible employees
  • ERISA: Group life is a welfare benefit plan subject to ERISA reporting, disclosure, and fiduciary requirements. Governmental, church, and fully voluntary (employee-paid) plans are exempt.

Group Health Insurance: Funding Methods & Tax Treatment

−
  • Fully-Insured: Employer pays premium to carrier; carrier bears all risk; subject to state insurance regulations; most common for small employers
  • Self-Funded (ASO): Employer bears all claims risk; uses TPA for administration; stop-loss insurance for catastrophic claims; exempt from most state insurance regulations under ERISA preemption; common for large employers
  • Level-Funded (Hybrid): Fixed monthly payment; refund if claims are low; stop-loss protection; growing with mid-size employers
  • VEBA (IRC §501(c)(9)): Voluntary Employees' Beneficiary Association — tax-exempt trust established by employer or through collective bargaining. Employer contributions deductible when made to the trust.

Group Disability, Workers' Comp & Other Benefits

−
  • Group STD: Benefit period up to 6 months; typically 60-70% of salary; short or no elimination period
  • Group LTD: Begins after STD; benefit period to age 65 (or specified years). Employer-paid = taxable benefits. Employee-paid = tax-free benefits.
  • Group LTD social security offset: Group LTD policies often reduce benefits by SSDI amount received

Weekly sum to surviving spouse for life or until remarriage; state-specific

WC benefits are generally tax-free to the employee. Employer premiums are deductible. State-mandated program; no deductibles or coinsurance for injured employees.

  • Unemployment Insurance: Joint federal-state; funded by employer payroll taxes (FUTA + SUTA). Benefits taxable to recipient. Typically up to 26 weeks.
  • Educational Assistance (IRC §127): Up to $5,250/year excludable from employee income for undergraduate AND graduate education; no job-relation requirement
  • Group LTCI: Generally less expensive than individual; premiums deductible to employer, not taxable to employee; benefits tax-free up to indexed limits

Business Risk Exposures & Key Coverages

−
  • BOP (Business Owner's Policy): Packages property + general liability + business income/interruption + extra expense. Cost-effective for small-to-mid-size businesses.
  • General Liability: BI and PD to third parties from premises, operations, products, or completed operations
  • Professional Liability (E&O / Malpractice): Claims from errors, omissions, or negligence in professional services
  • EPLI (Employment Practices Liability): Wrongful termination, harassment, discrimination claims. Critical for employers.
  • D&O (Directors & Officers): Protects board members and officers from personal liability for management decisions
  • Cyber Liability: Data breach response, ransomware, forensic investigation, business interruption. Growing critical importance.
  • Workers' Comp: State-mandated; no deductible for employees; benefits include medical, disability, death, rehabilitation

Buy-Sell Agreements EXAM

−
Cross-Purchase
Each owner buys policy on every other owner. Surviving owner gets stepped-up cost basis. Requires N×(N−1) policies. Better for small number of owners.
Entity Redemption
Company buys policy on each owner; redeems deceased's interest. N policies needed (simpler). Surviving owners get NO step-up in basis. C-corp: AMT risk on policy proceeds.
  • Disability buy-sell: Triggered after 12-24 month total disability; funded with disability insurance; long EP confirms permanent disability
  • Valuation: Agreed value (risk of being stale), book value formula, earnings multiple, or independent appraisal
  • Life-funded buy-sell: Death benefit pays buyout — ensures liquidity. Premiums NOT deductible. Death benefit generally tax-free.

Key Person Insurance & BOE Disability

−
  • Key Person Life Insurance: Company-owned, company-beneficiary policy on an essential employee. Replaces lost revenue; covers replacement costs. Premiums NOT deductible. Death benefit income-tax-free (COLI notice requirements apply).
  • Business Overhead Expense (BOE) Disability: Covers FIXED operating costs (rent, utilities, employee salaries, loan payments) while owner is disabled. Benefit period: 1-2 years. Premiums are deductible. Benefits are taxable. For small professional practices only, NOT large corporations.
  • Disability Buyout Insurance: Funds buy-sell agreement triggered by long-term disability. Elimination period typically 12-24 months.

Non-Qualified Plans, Executive Bonus & LIRP

−
  • NQDC (Non-Qualified Deferred Compensation): Unfunded promise to pay future benefit. No immediate deduction for employer; taxable to employee when received. No contribution limits or nondiscrimination rules. Funded informally with COLI or annuities. Risk: employer insolvency means employee is an unsecured creditor.
  • Executive Bonus Plan (IRC §162): Employer pays bonus equal to insurance premium on executive's personal policy. Bonus is deductible to employer; taxable to executive. Executive owns the policy and controls the cash value. Restriction agreement creates “golden handcuffs.”
  • SERP (Supplemental Executive Retirement Plan): Employer-funded supplemental retirement for key executives; informally funded with COLI; no ERISA limits; highly customizable.
  • LIRP (Life Insurance Retirement Plan): Permanent life insurance (whole, UL, VUL) accumulates tax-deferred cash value for retirement income. Tax-deferred growth; tax-free policy loans/withdrawals (up to basis). Must avoid MEC status. Not subject to ERISA or §415 limits. Death benefit provides succession liquidity.
LIRP Example (Sara): Pays $200,000 in premiums by age 65; cash value grows to $650,000. Withdraws $20,000/year tax-free (up to basis), then takes policy loans for remainder — still tax-free if policy remains in force.

State vs. Federal Regulatory Framework

−
  • Insurance is primarily state-regulated under McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945): federal authority limited to antitrust and fair labor; states carry the major burden
  • NAIC: All 50 state insurance commissioners. Develops model laws. Has NO enforcement power; cannot force adoption. Accreditation program incentivizes state consistency to maintain state-level regulation.
  • Key Federal Laws: ERISA (employee benefit plans), COBRA (continuation), FMLA (family leave), HIPAA (portability/privacy), ACA (essential benefits), Dodd-Frank (financial reform, SEC/FINRA oversight)

Regulatory Standards for Advisors EXAM

−
  • NAIC Suitability in Annuity Transactions Model Regulation: Producers must exercise reasonable diligence, care, and skill — act in consumer's best interest when recommending an annuity. Must assess financial situation, insurance needs, and objectives.
  • SEC Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI): Applies to broker-dealers recommending variable annuities to retail customers. Best-interest standard at time of recommendation; conflict disclosure (Form CRS); documentation required. Reg BI does NOT apply to fixed or fixed-indexed annuities (not classified as securities).
  • CFP Board Standards: Fiduciary duty (loyalty, care, follow client instructions). Full disclosure of material conflicts. Act in the client's best interest at all times.
  • FINRA Rule 2111 (Suitability): Does not require lowest-cost product, but does require that costs are reasonable and necessary given the client's profile.

Abusive Sales Practices

  • Churning: Repeatedly switching policies solely to generate commissions. Clients incur unnecessary fees, surrender charges, and coverage disruptions.
  • Twisting: Misrepresenting a competitor's policy to induce replacement. A form of churning.
  • Lack of transparency: Most common FINRA annuity complaint. Failure to fully explain all fees, restrictions, surrender charges, and disadvantages — not just collecting perfunctory disclosure signatures.
  • Free look period: Stronger consumer protection than a signed disclosure form — 10-30 days to cancel any annuity for full refund without penalty.
CFP® obligation: Adhere to fiduciary duty, disclose all material conflicts, document suitability analysis, and provide full cost transparency. Ethical conduct builds long-term client trust.
Annuity Performance Context: Annuities underperform in sustained bull markets due to caps, participation rate limitations, and expense charges. EIAs average 80-90% participation rates; caps typically 3-7%. Annuities are designed for longevity risk protection, NOT growth maximization. Compare annuities to annuities, not to brokerage accounts. The right question is: does this client need guaranteed income they cannot outlive?
